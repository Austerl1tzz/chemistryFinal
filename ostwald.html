The Ostwald Process

The Ostwald Process is one of the most common methods used for the industrial production of nitric acid (HNO₃). Developed by Wilhelm Ostwald in 1902, this process converts ammonia (NH₃) into nitric acid through a series of controlled oxidation reactions.

How Does It Work?

The Ostwald Process involves three main stages:

Stage 1: Primary Oxidation

Reaction: \[ 4NH_3(g) + 5O_2(g) \rightarrow 4NO(g) + 6H_2O(g) \] Conditions: - Temperature: 600–800 °C - Pressure: 4–10 atmospheres - Catalyst: Platinum-rhodium gauze Outcome: Ammonia is oxidized to nitric oxide (NO) and water vapor.

Stage 2: Secondary Oxidation

Reaction: \[ 2NO(g) + O_2(g) \rightarrow 2NO_2(g) \] Conditions: - Temperature: 50 °C Outcome: Nitric oxide (NO) is further oxidized to nitrogen dioxide (NO₂).

Stage 3: Absorption

Reaction: \[ 3NO_2(g) + H_2O(l) \rightarrow 2HNO_3(aq) + NO(g) \] Outcome: Nitrogen dioxide (NO₂) reacts with water to form dilute nitric acid (HNO₃).

Chemical Equations

The overall reaction for the Ostwald Process is:

\[ 4NH_3(g) + 5O_2(g) \rightarrow 4NO(g) + 6H_2O(g) \]

\[ 2NO(g) + O_2(g) \rightarrow 2NO_2(g) \]

\[ 3NO_2(g) + H_2O(l) \rightarrow 2HNO_3(aq) + NO(g) \]

Conditions and Optimization

The Ostwald Process is optimized to maximize efficiency and minimize side reactions. Here are the key conditions and optimization techniques:

1. Temperature Control

Primary Oxidation: - Optimal temperature: 600–800 °C. - Higher temperatures increase reaction rates but can degrade the catalyst. - Lower temperatures favor the formation of nitric oxide (NO) but reduce reaction rates.

Secondary Oxidation: - Optimal temperature: 50 °C. - Lower temperatures prevent the decomposition of nitrogen dioxide (NO₂).

2. Pressure

Primary Oxidation: - Optimal pressure: 4–10 atmospheres. - Higher pressures increase the yield of nitric oxide (NO) but require more energy. - Lower pressures reduce energy costs but decrease efficiency.

3. Catalyst

Platinum-Rhodium Gauze: - Composition: 90% platinum, 10% rhodium. - Role: Increases the rate of ammonia oxidation while minimizing side reactions. - Challenges: Catalyst degradation due to high temperatures and impurities.

4. Recycling

Nitric Oxide (NO): - The NO produced in the absorption stage is recycled back to the secondary oxidation stage. - This ensures efficient use of reactants and minimizes waste.
